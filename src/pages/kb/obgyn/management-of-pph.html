Management of Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH)

Last updated: July 12, 2025

Author(s): The HO Guide Team

Definition and Classification

Postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) is defined by excessive blood loss after childbirth and is classified based on its timing and severity.

Primary Postpartum Hemorrhage

Primary postpartum hemorrhage can be defined as:

  • Bleeding from the genital tract of 500mls or more within the first 24 hours of delivery following a vaginal delivery.
  • Bleeding from the genital tract of 1000mls or more within the first 24 hours of delivery following a caesarean delivery.
  • Bleeding from the genital tract of 1500mls or more within the first 24 hours of delivery following a caesarean hysterectomy.
  • Bleeding from the genital tract that causes a decrease in hematocrit by 10% or more within the first 24 hours of delivery.
  • Any degree of bleeding from the genital tract within the first 24 hours of delivery that is significant enough to affect the hemodynamic stability of the woman.

Primary PPH occurs within the first 24 hours after delivery. While traditionally defined by specific blood loss volumes, the modern clinical focus has shifted to a more functional definition that considers the patient's stability. The definitions include:

  • Quantitative Blood Loss: A cumulative blood loss of 1,000 ml or more, or bleeding associated with signs/symptoms of hypovolemia.
  • Clinical Signs of Instability: Any bleeding that affects the hemodynamic stability of the woman.
  • Change in Hematocrit: A decrease in hematocrit of 10% or more.

Secondary Postpartum Hemorrhage

Secondary PPH is abnormal bleeding occurring 24 hours after delivery until 12 weeks postpartum. It is usually caused by postpartum endometritis or retained products of conception.

The "4 Ts": Understanding the Etiology of PPH

A helpful mnemonic for identifying the causes of postpartum hemorrhage is the "4 Ts" — Tone, Trauma, Tissue, and Thrombin:

  • Tone (Uterine Atony): The most common cause (70–80% of cases). Occurs when the uterus fails to contract after delivery. Risk factors include uterine overdistension (e.g., multiple gestation, polyhydramnios, macrosomia), prolonged labor, and uterine relaxants.
  • Trauma: Lacerations of the cervix, vagina, perineum, or uterine rupture. Common after operative deliveries or precipitous labor.
  • Tissue: Retained placental fragments or clots prevent uterine contraction. May result from succenturiate lobes or incomplete placental delivery.
  • Thrombin: Coagulopathies impair clotting. These may be congenital (e.g., von Willebrand disease) or acquired (e.g., DIC from pre-eclampsia, placental abruption, or sepsis).

Prevention: Active Management of the Third Stage of Labor (AMTSL)

Proactive measures are paramount. The active management of the third stage of labor (AMTSL) is a proven set of interventions that can prevent the majority of PPH cases due to uterine atony.

The core components of AMTSL are:

  • Administration of a uterotonic agent: Oxytocin (10 IU IM/IV) is the preferred drug and should be given within one minute of the baby's birth.
  • Controlled cord traction (CCT): Gentle, sustained traction on the umbilical cord with counter-traction on the uterus facilitates placental delivery.
  • Uterine massage: Massaging the uterine fundus after placental delivery stimulates and maintains contractions.

While all components are traditionally taught, evidence shows that the administration of a uterotonic is the most critical step in preventing PPH.

A Systematic Approach to Managing Established PPH

When postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) occurs, a structured, multidisciplinary response is essential. Management is typically approached in three escalating stages:

Stage 1: Initial Response and Resuscitation

  • Call for Help: Notify the full obstetric, anesthetic, and nursing team. Communicate clearly with the patient and her family.
  • Resuscitate:
    • Secure two large-bore (14–16G) IV lines
    • Infuse warmed isotonic crystalloids rapidly
    • Administer high-flow oxygen
    • Send blood samples for CBC, coagulation profile, and crossmatch (≥4 units)
    • Continuously monitor vital signs
    • Insert a Foley catheter to monitor urine output and keep the bladder empty

Stage 2: Identify and Treat the Cause (The 4 Ts in Action)

While resuscitating, determine and address the cause of bleeding:

  • Tone (Uterine Atony):
    • Bimanual uterine massage
    • Administer uterotonics sequentially:
      • Oxytocin: 10–40 IU in 500–1000 mL IV infusion
      • Methylergonovine: 0.2 mg IM (contraindicated in hypertension/preeclampsia)
      • Carboprost: 0.25 mg IM every 15 min (max 8 doses; caution in asthma)
      • Misoprostol: 600–1000 mcg rectally, orally, or sublingually
  • Trauma:
    • Inspect the cervix, vagina, and perineum thoroughly
    • Repair all lacerations promptly
    • If uterine rupture is suspected (e.g. prior uterine surgery), prepare for laparotomy
  • Tissue (Retained Placenta):
    • Inspect placenta for completeness
    • Perform manual uterine exploration under anesthesia if fragments are suspected
  • Thrombin (Coagulopathy):
    • Tranexamic Acid (TXA): 1g IV over 10 minutes, repeatable after 30 minutes if needed
    • If massive hemorrhage, activate a 1:1:1 massive transfusion protocol (RBC:FFP:platelets)

Stage 3: Refractory PPH – Mechanical and Surgical Interventions

  • Uterine Balloon Tamponade: Insert and inflate a Bakri balloon to apply intrauterine pressure and control bleeding.
  • Uterine Compression Sutures: Apply B-Lynch or similar sutures during laparotomy to compress the uterus and control atony-related bleeding.
  • Vascular Ligation: Surgically ligate uterine or internal iliac arteries to reduce uterine perfusion.
  • Hysterectomy: As a life-saving last resort, subtotal or total hysterectomy may be required if bleeding persists despite all other measures.

The 'HAEMOSTASIS' Mnemonic

H - Help:
Call for experienced help immediately.
A - Assess & Resuscitate:
Simultaneously check vitals (ABC), secure IV access, and begin fluid resuscitation.
E - Etiology & Empty bladder:
Determine the cause (using "4 Ts") and ensure the bladder is empty.
M - Massage:
Perform uterine massage.
O - Oxytocics:
Administer uterotonic drugs systematically.
S - Shift to Theater:
If bleeding is uncontrolled, do not delay moving to an operating theater.
T - Tamponade:
Use an intrauterine balloon tamponade.
A - Apply compression Sutures:
If tamponade fails, apply uterine compression sutures.
S - Systematic pelvic devascularization:
Ligate vessels supplying the uterus.
I - Interventional Radiography:
Where available, perform uterine artery embolization.
S - Subtotal/Total Hysterectomy:
The final, life-saving step.

Post-Hemorrhage Care

Management does not end when the bleeding stops. Patients require close monitoring for at least 24 hours. Key considerations include managing postpartum anemia and providing psychological support for the patient and her family. Adhering to a systematic approach can effectively manage this obstetric emergency and significantly reduce the risk of maternal mortality.
