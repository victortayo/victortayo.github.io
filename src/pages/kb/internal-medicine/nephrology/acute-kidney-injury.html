Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)

Last updated: July 24, 2025

Author(s): Dr. Gemini

1.0 Introduction

Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) is a clinical syndrome characterized by an abrupt decline in glomerular filtration rate (GFR), leading to the accumulation of nitrogenous waste products and dysregulation of fluid, electrolyte, and acid-base homeostasis. While a global health issue, the incidence and etiological spectrum of AKI in sub-Saharan Africa, and specifically Nigeria, present unique challenges. The triad of infectious diseases, unregulated exposure to nephrotoxins, and high rates of obstetric and trauma-related complications creates a distinct epidemiological landscape compared to high-income countries. This review aims to provide a comprehensive, technically-focused overview of the pathophysiology, diagnosis, and management of AKI, tailored to the realities of clinical practice in Nigeria.

2.0 Definition and Staging

The contemporary definition of AKI relies on the consensus-driven Kidney Disease: Improving Global Outcomes (KDIGO) criteria, which standardize diagnosis based on changes in serum creatinine (sCr) and urine output.

2.1 KDIGO Diagnostic Criteria

An AKI diagnosis is established if any of the following are met:

  • An increase in sCr by ≥0.3 mg/dL (≥26.5 µmol/L) within a 48-hour period.
  • An increase in sCr to ≥1.5 times a known or presumed baseline value within the preceding 7 days.
  • A reduction in urine volume to <0.5 mL/kg/hour for a duration of 6 hours.

2.2 KDIGO Staging of Severity

The severity of AKI is stratified into three stages, which correlate with patient outcomes.

Stage Serum Creatinine Criteria Urine Output Criteria
1 1.5–1.9 times baseline OR ≥0.3 mg/dL increase <0.5 mL/kg/h for 6-12 hours
2 2.0–2.9 times baseline <0.5 mL/kg/h for ≥12 hours
3 ≥3.0 times baseline OR Increase in sCr to ≥4.0 mg/dL (353.6 µmol/L) OR Initiation of Renal Replacement Therapy (RRT) <0.3 mL/kg/h for ≥24 hours OR Anuria for ≥12 hours

3.0 Pathophysiology and Etiological Classification

AKI is mechanistically classified into three categories: pre-renal, intrinsic renal, and post-renal.

3.1 Pre-Renal AKI

This is a functional response to renal hypoperfusion without parenchymal damage. Reduced renal blood flow triggers intra-renal autoregulatory mechanisms, including afferent arteriolar vasodilation (via prostaglandins) and efferent arteriolar vasoconstriction (via angiotensin II), to maintain GFR. When systemic hemodynamics are severely compromised, these mechanisms fail.

Etiologies in Nigeria:

  • Absolute Volume Depletion: Severe dehydration from gastroenteritis (e.g., cholera, rotavirus), haemorrhagic shock (road traffic accidents, postpartum haemorrhage).
  • Reduced Effective Circulating Volume: Distributive shock from sepsis (e.g., malaria, typhoid, pneumonia) is a leading cause. Systemic vasodilation and capillary leak reduce central blood volume. Other causes include advanced cardiac failure and hepatorenal syndrome.
  • Pharmacological: NSAIDs inhibit prostaglandin synthesis, impairing afferent vasodilation. ACE inhibitors and ARBs block angiotensin II, causing efferent vasodilation and reducing filtration pressure.

3.2 Intrinsic AKI

This involves direct structural damage to the kidney parenchyma.

  • Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN): The most common form of intrinsic AKI, resulting from either ischemia or nephrotoxicity.
    • Ischemic ATN: Represents the endpoint of severe or prolonged pre-renal AKI.
    • Nephrotoxic ATN: Caused by substances like aminoglycosides, traditional herbal preparations ("Agbo"), heavy metals, radiocontrast media, myoglobin (rhabdomyolysis), and hemoglobin (hemolysis).
  • Acute Glomerulonephritis (AGN): Immune-mediated inflammation of the glomeruli.
  • Acute Interstitial Nephritis (AIN): Inflammation of the renal interstitium, typically from drugs or infections.

3.3 Post-Renal AKI

Caused by obstruction of the urinary tract. Increased hydrostatic pressure in Bowman's space opposes glomerular filtration.

Etiologies:

  • Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH) and prostate carcinoma are the most frequent causes in men.
  • In women, advanced cervical cancer is a key cause.
  • Bilateral ureteric stones or a blocked urethral catheter are other important considerations.

4.0 Clinical Evaluation and Diagnosis

  • History and Physical Examination: Focus on drug history (including herbal remedies), volume status, and signs of urinary obstruction.
  • Urinalysis: A critical, non-invasive tool.
    • Pre-renal: Bland sediment, high specific gravity, low FENa <1%.
    • ATN: Muddy brown granular casts, Isosthenuria, and FENa >2%.
    • AGN: Red blood cell casts, dysmorphic red cells, and proteinuria.
  • Biochemistry: Serial measurement of electrolytes, urea, and creatinine is essential.
  • Renal Ultrasonography: Essential for ruling out post-renal obstruction (hydronephrosis).

5.0 Management Principles

Management is multifaceted, focusing on reversing the underlying cause and providing supportive care.

  • Hemodynamic Optimization: Prompt and adequate volume resuscitation with isotonic crystalloids.
  • Discontinuation of Nephrotoxins: All potentially offending agents must be stopped immediately.
  • Relief of Obstruction: Urgent urinary catheterization or nephrostomy tube placement.
  • Management of Complications:
    • Hyperkalemia: An acute medical emergency. Managed with IV calcium gluconate, insulin-dextrose, beta-2 agonists, and potentially RRT.
    • Metabolic Acidosis: Severe acidemia (pH < 7.1) may require intravenous sodium bicarbonate.
    • Volume Overload: Managed with fluid restriction and high-dose loop diuretics.

6.0 Indications for Renal Replacement Therapy (RRT)

The "AEIOU" mnemonic serves as a clinical guide for the initiation of urgent RRT:

  • A - Acidosis: Severe metabolic acidosis (e.g., pH < 7.1) refractory to medical therapy.
  • E - Electrolytes: Refractory and severe hyperkalemia (e.g., K+ > 6.5 mmol/L with ECG changes).
  • I - Ingestions: Dialyzable toxins (e.g., methanol, ethylene glycol, salicylates).
  • O - Overload: Refractory volume overload causing pulmonary edema.
  • U - Uremia: Symptomatic uremia (encephalopathy, pericarditis, intractable bleeding).

7.0 Conclusion and Future Directions

Acute Kidney Injury poses a substantial public health burden in Nigeria. Prompt recognition, rapid identification of the etiology, and systematic management are critical. Future efforts must focus on public health initiatives to reduce nephrotoxin exposure, improve primary care, and enhance access to diagnostics and RRT.
